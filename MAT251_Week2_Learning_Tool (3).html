Spaced retrieval — free-response

Close your notes. Write your answer before revealing. The struggle is the learning.

6.1 recall: Write the area-between-curves formula from memory. What condition must hold for it to work directly?

A = ∫ₐᵇ [f(x) − g(x)] dx, where f(x) ≥ g(x) on [a,b]. If they cross, split at each crossing point and use |f − g| on each sub-interval.

6.2 recall: In words (not formula): how does the disk method build a solid of revolution? What is the volume of one disk?

Each disk is a thin circular slice perpendicular to the axis of rotation. Radius = f(x), thickness = dx. Volume of one disk ≈ π[f(x)]² dx. Integrate to sum all disks: V = π∫[f(x)]² dx.

6.3 recall: Write the arc length formula from memory. Explain why f′(x) appears inside the square root.

L = ∫ₐᵇ √(1 + [f′(x)]²) dx. The f′(x) comes from the Pythagorean theorem on tiny segments: ds² = dx² + dy², and dy = f′(x)dx, so ds = √(1 + [f′(x)]²) dx.

Mixed 6.1–6.2: Find the area between y = x² and y = x on [0,1]. Then set up (don't solve) the washer integral when this region is rotated about the x-axis.

Area: ∫₀¹(x − x²)dx = 1/6.
Washer: V = π∫₀¹[x² − x⁴]dx. Outer radius R = x, inner radius r = x², since x ≥ x² on [0,1].

Conceptual: Why does the washer method use R² − r² rather than (R − r)²? Sketch it.

Washer area = πR² − πr² (outer disk minus inner disk). (R−r)² is a solid disk with radius equal to the gap — a completely different shape. Subtract after squaring, never before.

Transfer (interleaved with Ch.5): Region bounded by y = √x, y = 0, x = 4. Use the shell method to find the volume when rotated about the y-axis.

V = 2π∫₀⁴ x√x dx = 2π∫₀⁴ x^(3/2) dx = 2π·[2x^(5/2)/5]₀⁴ = 2π·(64/5) = 128π/5.

Synthesis: How are the area formula (6.1), disk/washer formula (6.2), and arc length formula (6.3) all the same idea applied differently? What is the underlying pattern?

All three slice a quantity into infinitesimally thin pieces, express each piece as a simple formula, then integrate to sum across the interval. Area → thin rectangles. Volume → thin disks or shells. Arc length → tiny Pythagorean segments. Integration is always "add up infinitely many infinitely small pieces."
